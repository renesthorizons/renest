Why Saving During Your School Years is a Game-Changer

TL;DR: Why Students MUST Save During School

Don't wait until you graduate; saving as a student gives you a huge head start and protects your future.
1
Time is Your Ally: Starting early means your money grows more, and you'll need to save less later.
2
Beat Uncertainty: Savings protect against unexpected job markets, illnesses, or new opportunities (like travel or startups).
3
Control Your Present: Saving now is one of the only actions you can control, building financial skills and confidence.
4
Budget Smart: Categorize expenses (Necessities, Progress Costs), save 10% of the remainder, then cover Electives.
5
Use Resources: Leverage work-study, food banks, SNAP, health marketplace, and campus health supplies. Consider a 529 account (the "student 401k").
Saving in school isn't about pessimism; it's about creating freedom and opportunity for your future self.

Many believe that serious financial planningâ€”retirement, taxes, budgetingâ€”is best left for post-graduation, once a 'real' job and income materialize. The perception is that a few months of professional earnings will far outweigh any savings accumulated during school. This view overlooks crucial financial principles, life's inherent unpredictabilities and the whole point of saving, resulting in a lesser control over your future.

The Unbeatable Advantage: Time

One of the most compelling reasons to start saving as a student is the power of time in growing your money. The earlier you begin, even with small amounts, the less you'll need to save each year after that to reach significant financial goals. That means you literally need less money to hit the same goals for the rest of your life.

Preparing for an Unpredictable Future

The future is inherently uncertain. You don't know precisely what job you'll land, how the market might shift, or if innovations could alter your chosen field. Unexpected life events, such as illness or needing to support a family member, can also arise.

Saving now acts as a hedge, protecting you from these "black swan" events. It also empowers you to seize positive opportunities, like traveling after graduation before starting a job, or having the financial runway to explore a startup idea. This isn't about betting against your future success or being pessimistic; it's about creating more freedom and protecting yourself from factors beyond your control. I like to think of it as not putting all my eggs in one basket and giving myself more options that I might not even know I want to take right now. (thank goodness I had been saving by then time I decided to do a startup)

Taking Control and Building Lifelong Skills

While you can't control the future, you can control your actions in the present. Choosing to save, optimize spending, or leverage available financial benefits nowâ€”even if the amounts seem smallâ€”are the only things that are real and in your control.

This process also builds invaluable financial skills and confidence. Overcoming the initial inertia of managing money makes future financial decisions easier, as your brain becomes more adept at recognizing opportunities and making sound choices. Furthermore, financial stability can position you to help others and become a trusted resource within your friends and family.

The perceived stress and time cost of financial knowledge is often intimidating. However, the benefits don't come when you are an expert; they come as you learn and grow at your own pace. You will be rewarded with less stress, more confidence and control of your life.

Practical Steps to Start Saving as a Student

So, what are some ways to begin your savings journey? Here are several key areas to focus on:

1. Master Your Budget

Start by understanding your spending. Categorize your expenses:

  • Bucket 1: Necessities: Groceries (not food in generatl), rent.
  • Bucket 2: Progress Costs: Expenses directly related to your studies or work being possible (e.g., tuition, transport to class/job).
  • Save 10% of what remains
  • Bucket 3: Elective Expenses: Everything else.

The goal is to minimize the first two categories to maximize funds available for the third. Crucially, before allocating money to elective spending, aim to save at least 10% of what remains after covering necessities and progress cost. This becomes easier over time as your brain adjusts to your budgeted amounts and you always have a budget to check when you need.

2. Leverage Campus and Community Resources

Many resources are available specifically for students:

  • Campus Work-Study: Often readily available and designed to fit a student schedule.
  • Food Banks: Both campus and local food banks are there to support students.
  • Government Assistance: Investigate if you qualify for programs like SNAP (food assistance) and associated benefits like Medicaid or discounted WiFi.
  • Health Insurance Marketplace: Explore options offered by your state.
  • On-Campus Health Supplies: Student health centers or other campus locations often provide highly discounted or free supplies like Plan B, condoms, ibuprofen, allergy medicine, and thermometers.

While credit cards can be very effective for managing expenses and earning rewards, it's vital to understand them thoroughly. We'll delve into credit card strategies in a future article.

3. The 529 Account: A Student's Financial PowerTool

For all students, we strongly recommend exploring a 529 account. This account uniquely combines the tax advantages often associated with retirement accounts, the flexibility of a checking account, and upfront benefits comparable to a cashback credit card.

A 529 plan can serve you from your freshman year through advanced degrees like med school or a PhD. Beyond your own education, it can also be used for your future children or other family members, and supports your in your retirement planning. Overall, a 529 account represents one of the most immediate and effective long-term financial decisions a student can make. This leads us to calling the 529 plan the student 401k.
